This website is no longer updated and has been replaced with a static copy. The Spatial History Project was active at Stanford University from 2007-2022, engaging in dozens of collaborative projects led by faculty, staff, graduate students, post-docs, visiting scholars and others at Stanford and beyond. More than 150 undergraduate students from more than a dozen disciplines contributed to these projects. In addition to a robust intellectual exchange built through these partnerships, research outputs included major monographs, edited volumes, journal articles, museum exhibitions, digital articles, robust websites, and dozens of lightweight interactive visualizations, mostly developed with Adobe Flash (now defunct). While most of those publications live on in other forms, the content exclusive to this website is preserved in good faith through this static version of the site. Flash-based content is partially available in emulated form using the Ruffle emulator.

Lawyers in Rio de Janeiro: Movement in the City Center, 1860-1889
Lawyers in Rio de Janeiro: Movement in the City Center, 1860-1889

Authors: Kathy Harris, Mithu Datta, Whitney Berry, and Zephyr Frank

City directories provide precious historical information regarding the occupations and addresses of professionals and other residents of Rio de Janeiro. These records, when analyzed in spatial terms, suggest that whereas lawyers were concentrated in certain blocks around the city center, particularly in blocks close to the financial district, their individual locations tended to shift significantly over relatively short periods of time. In terms of the category (lawyers), the pattern is one of clustering in a loosely defined space; in terms of individual locations (addresses occupied by lawyers), the pattern is one of constant movement.

Although this simple visualization cannot offer answers as to why lawyers clustered or why they were so seldom present in the same address for extended periods of time, it does help raise these questions in the first place. In particular, without paying close attention to the addresses and spatial locations of lawyers, it would be easy to read through the city directories and miss this overwhelming pattern of movement within a more or less constrained space. This finding is more important than it may seem at first glance. When characterizing lawyers in Rio de Janeiro during this period, it is now necessary to attend to the fact that they rarely stayed put in the same building. The urban scene was typified by movement, not stability. Social networks were not, it seems equally likely, primarily anchored in particular spaces, but also quite flexible and mobile. Density and general propinquity sufficed for lawyers. Fixed addresses were unnecessary.

In addition to helping raise questions about spatial distributions and movement, the visualization also calls attention to the way coverage in the city directory may have varied in quality and completeness over time. Note, for example, the increase in number of parcels associated with lawyers between 1860 and 1870. Depending on the time period and the number of city directories consulted, historians might come to quite different conclusions about the distribution of lawyers (or any other category of resident) and their movements or lack thereof within the space of the city.

CAVEATS:

1.The study area is limited to the downtown business district, essentially the streets to the east of the Rua do Ourives and north of Rua Sete de Setembro. Most lawyers were listed with addresses in this zone, but a smaller percentage could always be found in other parts of the city.

2. A small number of addresses could not be located.

3. The parcels are coded for the presence of any lawyer. Thus, a parcel might contain multiple lawyers. Moreover, the fact that a parcel is colored in consecutive time periods does not mean that the occupant is the same lawyer.
